Residual Distribution:
The Residual Distribution is the part of a decedent’s estate that is remaining after payment of all debts, expenses, statutory claims, taxes, and testamentary or specific gifts. Typically, the residual distribution is the largest distribution made by an estate, and is divided into equal or unequal shares (e.g. “I give the rest, remainder and residue of the estate in equal shares to my children, per stirpes”).
Distribution is to your spouse first and then in equal shares to your children, as many as survive you, per stirpes (per stirpes is a defined term that essentially means in equal sub-shares when passing through the generations). The principal of the estate will be held and administered in trust for kids with the principal subject to their right of withdrawal beginning at age 25. At age *21 the Trust starts to kick off a sort of allowance for the beneficiaries by mandating (subject to special distributions clauses) annual distribution of the net income of the trust. The Trustee(s) have the right to access trust principal at any time before or after these ages for the beneficiary’s health, education, maintenance and support.

Family Pot Residual Explanation:

Held in a “Children’s Trust” for all children until the youngest child turns 25 years old – this is called a “Family Pot” trust, which provides a better resource platform for raising younger children when there is a large age gap. Nevertheless, “advancements of principal” to older children can be made to pay for things like a child’s first wedding, or to start a business, or buy a home. When the youngest child turns 25 years old, the trust divides into equal shares for your children, per stirpes, and is subject to their written request for a withdrawal.

Bloodline Trust explainer

The residue of the trust will be held in a “Bloodline Trust” arrangement for the children and their issue. A bloodline trust is designed so that, after both spouses have passed, the trust is divided into separate shares for each child, rather than distributing assets outright. Each child’s share is held in a continuing trust created for that child’s benefit, instead of being given directly to them, for asset protection reasons. Initially, each child serves as trustee of their own trust, unless blocked according to the special distributions clause of the trust (for instance, in the case of substance abuse issues). This allows each child to manage and benefit from their share while keeping the assets protected from creditors, divorce, and outside claims. When a child passes away, their trust share continues for their own descendants, keeping the inheritance within the family bloodline. In this way, the trust is structured to benefit multiple generations while preserving family assets over time.
