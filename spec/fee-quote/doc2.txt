Revision of Existing Estate Plan

The Revision of Existing Estate Plan option applies when a client already has a completed estate plan — either prepared by Speedwell Law or another firm — and now wishes to make substantive updates or structural changes.

1️⃣ Purpose
This representation option covers the time and expertise required to:

Review existing documents,

Identify inconsistencies or outdated provisions,

Redesign portions of the plan, and

Draft new or amended instruments as needed.

It ensures the client receives a fully updated and internally consistent plan, not just piecemeal amendments.

2️⃣ Fee Structure
Minimum fee: $3,000

Overages: Billed at the assigned attorney’s hourly rate (depending on tier).

This hybrid pricing reflects the unpredictable nature of revisions — many plans require complete restatement once updates begin.

Script for explaining fees:
“Because revising an existing plan can range from straightforward updates to full redrafts, we start with a $3,000 minimum. If it turns out that only limited revisions are needed, you’ll stay within that amount. If your plan requires more extensive restructuring, any additional time is billed at your attorney’s hourly rate.”

3️⃣ When to Offer
Offer this representation option when:

The client already has a trust or will-based plan but needs changes, restatements, or modernizations, or

The client requests review of another firm’s documents for integration into Speedwell Law’s structure.

If the client does not yet have a formal plan, this option does not apply — proceed with the standard Will-Based or Trust-Based plan menu.

4️⃣ Internal Guidance
Always confirm which documents they have and when they were last updated.

If the client cannot provide copies before the consultation, note that document review time will be included in the hourly billing if overages apply.

Flag whether the existing plan was drafted by Speedwell Law or by an outside firm — this affects internal tracking and potential retention credit.

In summary:
This representation option begins at $3,000, with additional time billed hourly. It covers revisions to existing estate plans and ensures the finished product meets Speedwell Law’s quality standards.

Incapacity Planning Documents Only

The Standalone Incapacity Planning Documents option is for clients who:

Are not yet ready to complete a full estate plan, or

Already have an existing estate plan but need to update or refresh their incapacity documents.

This option ensures that a client’s trusted decision-makers have current, valid authority to act in financial and medical matters, even if their broader estate planning documents remain unchanged.

1️⃣ What’s Included
This representation includes preparation (or restatement) of:

Financial Power of Attorney (POA)

Advance Medical Directive (AMD)

HIPAA Authorization

Burial Power of Attorney (BPOA)

These documents empower chosen agents to act during incapacity and ensure the client’s financial, medical, and end-of-life preferences are clearly expressed and legally enforceable.

2️⃣ Fee and Scope
Flat Fee: $1,795

Covers consultation, design, drafting, and execution coordination.

For clients refreshing existing documents, this replaces rather than supplements their prior POA/AMD set.

Does not include updates to trusts, wills, or asset-distribution provisions.

Optional: if the client later pursues a full estate plan within six months, the firm may, at its discretion, apply a partial credit toward that engagement.

3️⃣ When to Offer
Offer this representation option when:

The client’s current POA or AMD is more than 5 years old, or was executed under another state’s law.

They say their agent has changed (e.g., divorce, remarriage, new adult child).

They have an existing will or trust that is otherwise satisfactory but want their incapacity documents reviewed and refreshed.

They express that they “just need to update the Power of Attorney or Medical Directive.”

Suggested phrasing:
“This option is for clients who either don’t need a full estate plan right now or already have one but want to make sure their decision-making documents are current and enforceable. It gives you up-to-date protection without having to redo your whole plan.”

4️⃣ Internal Guidance
Always ask whether the client already has an estate plan and when their POA and AMD were last updated.

If they have prior documents, request copies before scheduling the attorney meeting.

Flag whether the documents were prepared in another state — those almost always need replacement.

Reassure the client that refreshing these documents is normal and proactive, not an indication that their prior plan was deficient.

In summary:
$1,795 flat fee; covers drafting or restatement of all incapacity documents.
Ideal for clients who are not yet ready for a full estate plan or who simply need to update existing POA and AMD documents.

Enhanced Creditor Protections ($195)

The Enhanced Creditor Protections feature adds special language to the trust giving the trustee additional powers to protect beneficiaries who might later face problems such as substance abuse, incapacity (for example, from brain injury or congenital conditions), divorce, lawsuits, or creditor claims.

Without this feature, the trust still provides a baseline level of protection, but the trustee has less flexibility to intervene if a beneficiary experiences one of these issues. With the enhancement, the trustee can pause or redirect distributions and, when appropriate, hold funds in a separate protective trust for that beneficiary’s health, maintenance, or treatment until the risk passes.

This feature is generally recommended whenever a living person will inherit from the trust.
If the client’s plan leaves everything to charity, there’s no need to include it — you can mention that omitting this feature will reduce their fee quote slightly.

In short, this is the clause that allows the trustee to:

Protect an heir with substance abuse or mental health struggles,

Delay or condition distributions during divorce or litigation,

Maintain public-benefit eligibility for a special-needs heir, and

Prevent depletion of the trust by creditors or poor financial judgment.

Guidance for the intake call:
“This is the language that gives your trustee the power to protect your loved ones if life throws them a curveball — addiction, lawsuits, or health problems. Most clients include it, because nearly every family has someone who could benefit from that flexibility.”

Blended Family ($500)

The Blended Family Enhancement applies when the client or their spouse has children from prior relationships, or when there are differing intended beneficiaries between spouses.

This enhancement addresses the added complexity of designing two coordinated estate plans under one household — ensuring both sides’ wishes are honored and protected without conflict.

1️⃣ When to Offer
Offer this feature when:

Either spouse has children from a previous relationship, or

The spouses have different intended distributions of their estates (e.g., “mine to my kids, yours to yours”), or

The client expresses concern about protecting children from a first marriage or keeping certain assets separate.

Skip this enhancement if both spouses agree that all assets will pass equally to all children collectively, treating them as one unified family.

2️⃣ How to Explain to the Client
“When there’s a blended family, it’s a bit like creating two estate plans in one. You each have your own set of wishes, and we need to make sure both are properly documented and coordinated so there’s no conflict later.

Instead of charging a whole second estate plan fee — which could easily be another four thousand dollars — we just apply a $500 enhancement to cover the extra design and drafting time.”

3️⃣ Decision Guidance
Confirm the distribution intent (shared vs. separate).

If they’re unsure, err on the side of including the enhancement — Misha can adjust it after reviewing their design preferences.

Note any blended-family dynamics in the client’s Lawmatics file for the design attorney.

In summary:
$500 enhancement; required when spouses have children from prior relationships or differing distribution goals.
