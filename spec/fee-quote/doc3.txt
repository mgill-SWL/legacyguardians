Living Trust Upgrade

1️⃣ Start the Conversation
Ask:

“Were you interested in creating a living trust as part of your estate plan?”

Wait for their response.

If they already say yes or clearly know they want a trust, skip this section and move directly to discussing trust tiers and service features.

2️⃣ If the Client Seems Will-Oriented
If you sense the client is leaning toward a will-based plan, gently educate them before quoting prices.

“Would you like me to explain the difference between a will and a trust?”

If they agree, continue:

“A trust isn’t the only way to avoid probate — but for most families, it’s usually the best option.

Think of it as a value proposition: it costs more upfront to create, but it saves your loved ones far more in time, expense, and stress later. It also gives you better protection for your family after you’re gone.”

Pause briefly to let that sink in.

3️⃣ When a Will Is a Good Fit
“There are a few cases where a simple will still makes sense — for example, when everything will be left equally to your kids, you’d be comfortable with them serving together as executors, and you don’t own any property outside of Virginia.

In those situations, a will plan can still accomplish your goals effectively, and we can absolutely help with that.”

(If they fit this profile, proceed to your will-based quote flow.)

4️⃣ When to Recommend the Trust Plan
If the client has multiple properties, blended family dynamics, minor children, out-of-state assets, or simply wants to make things easier for loved ones:

“For most families, the living trust plan provides the best overall protection. It avoids court involvement, keeps things private, and allows your trustee to manage or distribute assets quickly and smoothly. That’s why most of our clients ultimately choose the trust-based plan.”

Then transition into trust-tier pricing and SERVICE FEATURES (Enhanced Creditor Protections, Incapacity Documents, etc.).

Scenario	Coaching Note
Client already knows they want a trust	Skip the comparison and affirm their decision: “Perfect — that’ll give you the most flexibility and protection. Let’s go through your options.”
Client unsure or says ‘I just need a simple will’	Don’t push — teach. Keep it calm and educational: “That might be fine. Let’s talk through what makes a will the right choice versus when a trust pays for itself.”
Client starts focusing on cost	Reframe toward family benefit: “That’s understandable. Most people don’t realize that the cost of probate often ends up being several times more than the cost of setting up a trust. But even beyond the money — imagine the time, stress, and heartache your family would save by being able to skip the court process when they’re already grieving. Setting up the trust now makes things simple and peaceful for them later.
Client with complex assets or blended family	Use specific examples to reinforce: “In your case, since you have the second home and multiple beneficiaries, a trust really simplifies things for your loved one.”
Client is skeptical about trusts	Stay neutral and factual — no fear tactics. “It’s really about control. A will transfers control to the court; a trust keeps it in your loved one's hands.”

Most clients should have this feature included.

In summary:
This feature completes the estate plan by covering incapacity events. It’s standard for comprehensive protection and should usually be selected.

Enhanced Creditor Protections ($195)

The Enhanced Creditor Protections feature adds special language to the trust giving the trustee additional powers to protect beneficiaries who might later face problems such as substance abuse, incapacity (for example, from brain injury or congenital conditions), divorce, lawsuits, or creditor claims.

Without this feature, the trust still provides a baseline level of protection, but the trustee has less flexibility to intervene if a beneficiary experiences one of these issues. With the enhancement, the trustee can pause or redirect distributions and, when appropriate, hold funds in a separate protective trust for that beneficiary’s health, maintenance, or treatment until the risk passes.

This feature is generally recommended whenever a living person will inherit from the trust.
If the client’s plan leaves everything to charity, there’s no need to include it — you can mention that omitting this feature will reduce their fee quote slightly.

In short, this is the clause that allows the trustee to:

Protect an heir with substance abuse or mental health struggles,

Delay or condition distributions during divorce or litigation,

Maintain public-benefit eligibility for a special-needs heir, and

Prevent depletion of the trust by creditors or poor financial judgment.

Guidance for the intake call:
“This is the language that gives your trustee the power to protect your loved ones if life throws them a curveball — addiction, lawsuits, or health problems. Most clients include it, because nearly every family has someone who could benefit from that flexibility.”

Blended Family ($500)

The Blended Family Enhancement applies when the client or their spouse has children from prior relationships, or when there are differing intended beneficiaries between spouses.

This enhancement addresses the added complexity of designing two coordinated estate plans under one household — ensuring both sides’ wishes are honored and protected without conflict.

1️⃣ When to Offer
Offer this feature when:

Either spouse has children from a previous relationship, or

The spouses have different intended distributions of their estates (e.g., “mine to my kids, yours to yours”), or

The client expresses concern about protecting children from a first marriage or keeping certain assets separate.

Skip this enhancement if both spouses agree that all assets will pass equally to all children collectively, treating them as one unified family.

2️⃣ How to Explain to the Client
“When there’s a blended family, it’s a bit like creating two estate plans in one. You each have your own set of wishes, and we need to make sure both are properly documented and coordinated so there’s no conflict later.

Instead of charging a whole second estate plan fee — which could easily be another four thousand dollars — we just apply a $500 enhancement to cover the extra design and drafting time.”

3️⃣ Decision Guidance
Confirm the distribution intent (shared vs. separate).

If they’re unsure, err on the side of including the enhancement — Misha can adjust it after reviewing their design preferences.

Note any blended-family dynamics in the client’s Lawmatics file for the design attorney.

In summary:
$500 enhancement; required when spouses have children from prior relationships or differing distribution goals.
